The likely user breakdown:
The primary audience is probably a mix weighted toward street/daily enthusiasts and weekend warriors — people who drive their car every day but want it to be faster, louder, and more fun. These are the folks buying cold air intakes, cam packages, exhaust systems, and maybe a Stage 1 or Stage 2 build. They're the volume customers. They browse Instagram, watch YouTube pulls, and impulse-buy parts at midnight. The site needs to speak to them with exciting imagery, clear fitment info, and an easy shopping experience.
The secondary audience is the serious build crowd — people commissioning full engine builds, turbo kits, or complete packages in the 700-1000+ HP range. These are higher-ticket, lower-volume customers. They're not impulse buying. They're researching for weeks, comparing shops, and reading every detail on the packages page. For them, the builds gallery, the detailed package breakdowns, and the testimonials are what close the deal.
Local vs. national — it's almost certainly both, but the site architecture should reflect the difference. Parts and bolt-on kits ship nationally — that's the e-commerce play. But full engine builds, installations, and dyno tuning require the car to be at the shop, so that side of the business is local/regional. The site needs to serve both without confusing either. A guy in Florida should be able to buy a cam kit and check out in three clicks. A guy 45 minutes from the shop should see the builds gallery and book a consultation.
